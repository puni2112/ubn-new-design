i The Brand

An old dispensary, kept honest.

UBalance Naturals compounds high-potency liquid Ayurvedic formulations from real botanicals. The Apothecary system speaks in the language of the original herbalist's bench — bottle green, aged paper, a hand-set label — without the costume. Heritage as a measure of care, not nostalgia.

№ i

Compounded

Made the slow way, from named botanicals in stated amounts. The label tells you exactly what is in the bottle.

№ ii

Sealed

Every batch is closed with the house seal — a mark of who made it and a promise that nothing was added.

№ iii

Enduring

Recipes that predate the brand by centuries. We keep the formula and improve only the proof behind it.

Founded on the herbalist's principle that a remedy should be named in full and measured in the open, UBalance Naturals carries an old craft into modern shelves. The bottle is green because the glass always was; the type is set with care because a label was once the only guarantee a maker could offer.

ii Logo

The mark stays. The bottle returns.

We keep the existing medallion wordmark — here it sits in deep bottle ink on aged paper, or gilded in foil-warm reverse on green. No recolor of the mark itself; the heritage lives in its surroundings — the rules, the seal, the clear space.

iii Color

Bottle green. Aged paper. A little gold.

The palette is warm and printed — bottle green at the base, sepia ink, paper the colour of age. One herb green carries interaction; antique gold is the seal, used flat and sparingly. No bright colour ever, no pure black, no white.

iv Typography

A serif that speaks, a grotesque that labels.

Gambetta — a premium contrast serif — sets all headlines and body, with its calligraphic italic carrying every accent word. Cabinet Grotesk handles small-cap labels, drawer tags and dosing lines. Figures are old-style, set in the serif. No monospace, no third voice.

v Buttons & CTA

Pressed, not rounded.

Controls are crisp printed rectangles (2px), set in tracked small-caps. Bottle green is the default action; the gold-bordered button is the single premium moment, like a foil-stamped coupon. Hover deepens the ink — no motion, no bounce.

vi Components

The label is the component.

The signature element is the apothecary label — a double-ruled, centred panel with the botanical name, its Latin binomial and the dose, gilt-cornered. Everything else is flat and rule-framed; ornament is a diamond between two hairlines, never a shadow.

vii Iconography

Engraved line. Round caps.

A single line-icon set in the manner of an old engraving — 1.5px stroke, no fill, round caps and joins on a 24px grid. Icons inherit currentColor and sit in rule-framed cells. Typographic ornaments — the fleuron and diamond — stand in for decoration. No emoji.

viii Photography

Lamplight on the workbench.

Product as it sits on the herbalist's bench — warm, low directional light, a soft vignette, dried botanicals and amber glass. Lightly aged in tone, never clinical. The bottle is the hero; the props are the evidence of craft. Text never sits on the image.

Treatment

Warm, low directional light with a soft vignette. Gently aged tone — sepia-leaning, rich, never grey or moody.

Crop

Clean rectangle, 2px radius, framed by a hairline rule. The vignette does the edge-work, not a heavy shadow.

Composition

Amber glass and dried botanicals on a worn surface — the bench tells the story of how it was made.

ix Voice & Tone

Plain words, well set.

Speak like a careful maker, not a marketer. Name the botanical, state the amount, let the craft show. The heritage is in the manner, not in archaic words — we are old-fashioned about honesty, modern about clarity. No hype, no exclamation.

We write

"Compounded by hand from named botanicals. 3,825 mg of actives, sealed in every bottle."

Calm, specific, made-by-someone. Real names, real amounts, the maker present in the sentence. Honest about what it is.

We avoid

"The ultimate game-changing miracle boost!!!"

No hype, no exclamation, no costume-drama "thee & thou", no emoji. Heritage is a standard of care, not a gimmick of language.
